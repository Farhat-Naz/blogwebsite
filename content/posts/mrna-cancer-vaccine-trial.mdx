## Personalized Medicine at Scale

The premise of personalized cancer vaccines sounds almost science-fictional: sequence a patient's tumor DNA, identify unique mutations (neoantigens) that distinguish cancer cells from healthy ones, design an mRNA vaccine tailored to that specific tumor, and inject it to train the immune system to hunt down the cancer.

Phase I data was promising. Phase II results, just published in *The New England Journal of Medicine*, are remarkable.

## Trial Design

**mRNA-4157/V940** is a lipid-nanoparticle mRNA vaccine encoding up to 34 personalized neoantigens identified by whole-exome sequencing of each patient's tumor biopsy. Combined with pembrolizumab (Keytruda), an established checkpoint inhibitor.

The trial enrolled 157 patients with stage III/IV melanoma after surgical resection.

## Results at 3 Years

| Endpoint | Vaccine + Pembro | Pembro alone |
|---|---|---|
| Recurrence-free survival | 74.8% | 55.6% |
| Distant metastasis-free | 79.4% | 62.2% |
| Tumor size reduction (measurable disease) | 78.1% | 51.3% |
| Grade 3+ adverse events | 14.6% | 10.2% |

The vaccine group showed statistically significant improvement on every primary and secondary endpoint.

## How It Works

Unlike traditional vaccines targeting pathogen proteins, mRNA-4157 encodes tumor-specific neoantigens — protein sequences that exist only because of the patient's unique cancer mutations. The immune system learns to recognize and destroy cells displaying these markers.

Crucially, healthy cells don't express these neoantigens, making the therapy highly targeted with minimal off-target toxicity.

## What Comes Next

Moderna has initiated Phase III trials in non-small cell lung cancer and bladder cancer. The manufacturing turnaround — from tumor biopsy to personalized vaccine — now takes **28 days**, down from 90 days in Phase I, making the approach logistically viable for broader deployment.

The FDA has granted Breakthrough Therapy designation.
