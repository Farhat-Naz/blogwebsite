## The Challenge of Watching Memory in Real Time

Neuroscientists have known for decades that the hippocampus plays a central role in forming new memories. But capturing the exact moment encoding happens — at the timescale of individual neuronal spikes — has required invasive recordings previously only possible in rodents.

## A Rare Window: Epilepsy Surgery Patients

A team from Johns Hopkins and UCSF leveraged a rare clinical opportunity: patients undergoing evaluation for epilepsy surgery who had high-density electrocorticography (ECoG) grids implanted across temporal, parietal, and frontal cortices.

During clinically idle periods, patients completed a picture-word association memory task. The team recorded from 4,096 electrodes simultaneously at 2 kHz — the highest spatial and temporal resolution human memory recording ever achieved.

## What They Found

Memory encoding unfolds in four phases within the first 800 milliseconds:

1. **0–120 ms**: Primary sensory cortices process the stimulus
2. **120–280 ms**: Prefrontal cortex "gates" whether the information is novel enough to encode
3. **280–550 ms**: Hippocampal CA3 generates a sparse index code
4. **550–800 ms**: CA1 broadcasts the index back to cortex, binding the memory

Items that were later remembered showed **gamma-band synchrony** between the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus during phase 2 — a clear neural signature of successful encoding.

## Clinical Implications

This "encoding signature" could be used to:
- Diagnose early memory impairment in Alzheimer's disease before behavioral symptoms
- Trigger closed-loop brain stimulation precisely when encoding is failing
- Guide electrode placement for memory prosthetics

> "We've been searching for the fingerprint of memory for 70 years. Now we can see it as it happens." — Dr. Michael Kahana, Penn

The dataset is being released to the research community via the BRAIN Initiative data archive.
