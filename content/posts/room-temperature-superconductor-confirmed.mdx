## A Field Burned Before

The history of room-temperature superconductor claims is littered with retractions and controversy. Ranga Dias's 2023 claims collapsed under allegations of data manipulation. So when a Korean team published new LuH₂N data in early 2026, the scientific community was understandably skeptical.

## Three Labs, Same Result

What changed the conversation: three independent groups — at ETH Zurich, Argonne National Laboratory, and RIKEN Japan — separately synthesized the compound and measured:

- **Zero electrical resistance** at 294 K (21°C) and 1.8 GPa pressure
- **Meissner effect** (magnetic field expulsion) confirmed by SQUID magnetometry
- **Isotope effect** consistent with phonon-mediated superconductivity

All three used different synthesis routes and different measurement setups. All three found the same critical temperature within 2 K of each other.

## Why 1.8 GPa Still Matters

1.8 gigapascals is roughly 18,000 atmospheres — far beyond ambient pressure, but achievable with diamond anvil cells and, crucially, with engineered thin-film deposition techniques that could embed the compound in mechanical structures.

The Argonne team demonstrated a thin-film version that maintains superconductivity at 1.4 GPa — within reach of engineered ceramic substrates.

## What This Enables

If the pressure barrier is solved:
- **Power grids** with zero transmission losses (currently ~6% of global electricity)
- **MRI machines** without liquid helium cooling
- **Maglev transportation** at a fraction of current cost
- **Quantum computers** that operate at room temperature

> "This is real. The Meissner effect doesn't lie. The question is no longer 'does it work' but 'can we make it practical.'" — Prof. Laura Greene, NHMFL

A coordinated international consortium has been formed to crack the pressure problem.
