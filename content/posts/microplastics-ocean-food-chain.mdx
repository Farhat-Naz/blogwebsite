## Beyond Individual Harm

Previous microplastics research has focused on effects in single species: reduced fertility in fish, inflammation in seabirds, toxin accumulation in shellfish. A landmark 5-year mesocosm study published in *Nature Ecology & Evolution* takes a radically different approach — examining whole ecosystem dynamics.

## The Mesocosm Experiment

The team at the Plymouth Marine Laboratory constructed 18 large ocean mesocosms (1,000-liter sealed marine systems) mimicking North Atlantic coastal conditions, introducing microplastic concentrations ranging from pristine ocean levels to moderate pollution levels observed near urban coasts.

Over 5 years, they tracked:
- Phytoplankton community composition
- Zooplankton behavior and population dynamics
- Small pelagic fish (anchovy proxies)
- Nutrient cycling rates

## Cascade Effects

The findings reveal a cascade:

| Level | Effect |
|---|---|
| Phytoplankton | Shifted community toward smaller, less nutritious species |
| Zooplankton | Reduced lipid storage (lower energy density) |
| Small fish | 34% lower growth rates despite similar caloric intake |
| Nutrient cycling | 28% reduction in biological carbon pump efficiency |

Critically, the carbon pump disruption means the ocean absorbs **less atmospheric CO₂** — a feedback loop that could accelerate climate change beyond current models.

## The Compounding Problem

"The energy loss at each trophic level compounds," explains lead author Dr. Rachel Steinberg. "A 15% reduction in zooplankton energy content translates to a 34% growth reduction in fish because the fish must spend more energy foraging."

## Policy Implications

At current pollution trajectories, the researchers warn these trophic disruptions could reduce global fishery yields by 8–12% by 2050, independent of climate and overfishing pressures.

The study is already being cited in ongoing UN plastic treaty negotiations.
