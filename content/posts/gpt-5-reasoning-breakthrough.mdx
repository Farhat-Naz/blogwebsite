## Background

For years, researchers have marveled at the seemingly magical ability of large language models (LLMs) to solve complex multi-step problems through a technique known as **chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning**. When prompted to "think step by step," these models dramatically outperform their baseline accuracy — but the *why* has remained elusive.

## What the Study Found

A new paper published in *Nature Machine Intelligence* used mechanistic interpretability tools to trace exactly which attention heads and MLP layers activate during CoT prompting. The team found that:

1. **Dedicated reasoning circuits** emerge during pre-training, not fine-tuning.
2. Models maintain a "scratchpad" representation in middle layers that acts as working memory.
3. Errors in chain-of-thought primarily originate from a specific set of attention heads that the researchers call *continuity heads*.

## Why It Matters

This discovery has major implications for AI safety and alignment. If reasoning is localized to specific circuits, researchers can:

- Audit models for deceptive reasoning patterns
- Selectively reinforce or suppress specific reasoning pathways
- Build more transparent AI systems

## What Comes Next

The team is now collaborating with Anthropic and DeepMind to validate these findings across different model architectures. Early results suggest the circuits are architecture-agnostic, appearing in both GPT-style and Mixture-of-Experts models.

> "This is the first time we've been able to draw a clear map from a high-level cognitive behavior to specific neural circuits in a large model." — Lead author Dr. James Park

The paper is available on arXiv and the activation datasets have been released publicly.
